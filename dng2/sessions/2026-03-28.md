# Uthodurn

:::summary
In which our bloodied and thoroughly disheveled heroes
arrive at the monumental gates of Uthodurn at a fashionably late hour,
only to discover that the mountain city’s welcoming committee
consists of ten crossbow-wielding sentries
whose enthusiasm for late-night visitors is exceedingly restrained.
Having convinced these gentlemen of their peaceful intentions,
the party is received by a hastily armored Commander
who absorbs the grim tidings of the caravan’s destruction
with the quiet displeasure
of a man roused from bed
to learn that someone has lost something irreplaceable,
and who dispatches them to a subterranean tavern
called the Broken Stool
with the exceedingly helpful instruction to “not talk to anyone,”
a directive that Doctor Pepe interprets
with characteristic flexibility
by immediately going invisible and skulking about the premises.
The descent of one hundred and fifty feet of spiraling staircase
into the mountain’s belly
proves an unexpectedly pleasant affair,
the brutal cold of the surface giving way by degrees
to the warmth of forges and the civilizing aroma of fresh bread,
culminating in the discovery of a vast and splendid cavern city
and the acquaintance of a delightfully surly dwarven innkeeper named Luda,
who dispenses gossip of impending war alongside generous bowls of stew.
The following morning brings the formidable challenge
of spending one’s hard-won gold before a midday appointment,
a task to which the party rises heroically,
dispersing across the Liber Disk
to commission enchanted weaponry from a flower-bedecked blacksmith
whose acquaintance with a local aasimar
piques Elara’s considerable interest,
and to endure the meticulous indignity
of being fitted for studded leather
by a fastidious dwarf
at an armory run by a bearded lady of entrepreneurial ambition.
These commercial endeavors
are duly curtailed by the appointment awaiting them at the Grand Disk,
where a magnificently composed Minister of the Diarchy
hears their account of the catastrophe,
confirms the potential involvement of the Kryn Dynasty
while noting the troubling inconsistency of undead among their forces,
pays the adventurers their seventy-five gold apiece,
bestows upon them the handsome title
of Friends of the Court,
and then poses the one question
that nobody present can satisfactorily answer:
how, precisely,
did the enemy know about a convoy that was supposed to be secret?
Thereafter the party retires to an eccentric antique shop
whose proprietress proves amenable to bartering luxury pelts for magical luggage
and who hints, with admirable directness,
that she may have future need of individuals
comfortable with the less conventional methods of acquisition.
:::

At the threshold of Uthodurn’s monumental gates,
Brennik Coalvein hauled himself upright
despite the grievous wounds still etched across his frame.
The young recruit cupped his hands around his mouth
and, in a voice made hoarse by days of marching,
announced himself as a Glassblade of Palebank Village,
desperately requesting entry
for the bloodied remnants of their diplomatic caravan.

A flurry of urgent activity erupted along the battlements.
Deep, resonant horns sounded a solemn note of entry,
and with a profound, earth-shaking rumble
of hidden gears and ancient counterweights,
the colossal doors began to part.
From the warm amber glow beyond,
no fewer than ten armored sentries emerged into the snow,
torches held high and crossbows leveled
with the sort of professional hostility
that suggested they were not in the habit
of receiving late-night visitors
on the strength of a shouted introduction alone.

Brennik’s halting explanation of the ambush
eventually persuaded the duty sergeant
to order the crossbows lowered.
The convoy was expected,
although they noted
the unfamiliar Flot goat
that was Scarlet in wild shape.
Once inside the semidomed courtyard,
she dropped her bestial guise,
resuming her natural halfling form
to the surprise and then visible relief of the sentries.
Kragor stepped forward and addressed the nearest guard,
recounting the devastation of the ambush in clipped, soldierly tones.
The party was told to hold their story and wait for the Commander.

## Commander Ironfell

The man who emerged from the barracks
was still fastening the buckles of his breastplate.
Commander Tormund Ironfell was a dwarf of late middle age,
broad as a forge anvil,
with an iron-grey beard trimmed short
and deep-set eyes that radiated
equal measures of competence and displeasure.
He strode past the adventurers without a glance,
making directly for young Brennik.

The exchange was brief and grim.
Brennik, overwhelmed by exhaustion and grief,
could only recount that the attack had been swift
and that he had been knocked unconscious
before the worst of the slaughter.
Ironfell’s jaw tightened as he absorbed the news.
He announced curtly
that he would need to consult with the palace
regarding the situation.
Then, fixing the adventurers with a look
that brooked no argument,
he directed them to an establishment
called the Broken Stool
and issued a single, emphatic instruction:
“Don’t talk to anyone.”

With that, the Commander dispatched Brennik home
and summoned a pair of Glassblades to serve as escorts.
As the party fell into step behind the guards,
Kragor drew alongside Therin
and quietly asked the dragonborn cleric
about the enchanted dagger recovered from the caravan wreckage.
Therin confirmed his earlier assessment:
the blade was of Aeorian make
and carried a modest but reliable enchantment.

## The Volition Disk

Their escorts led them
through the cavernous entry hall
and into a wide, vaulted archway,
beyond which a broad spiral staircase
descended into the living rock of the mountain.
This was the Auger Trail,
the great helical thoroughfare
that served as Uthodurn’s primary artery.

The descent was a full hundred and fifty feet,
and with each slow rotation downward,
the brutal cold of the surface
yielded incrementally to a welcome,
mineral-scented warmth.
The travelers loosened their furs and heavy cloaks,
breathing in the subterranean air
that carried the unmistakable fragrance
of burning pine, smoldering charcoal, and working forges,
layered beneath the more civilized aromas
of freshly baked bread and slow-cooked meat.
Therin, whose dragonborn constitution
had suffered the northern cold
with a barely concealed, reptilian misery,
visibly unburdened himself of several layers at once,
his heavy shoulders dropping
as the geothermal warmth seeped into his scales.

At last, the staircase opened
onto the vast expanse of the Volition Disk—
an enormous cavern one hundred and twenty feet high
and fifteen hundred feet across,
alive with the warm, flickering light
of countless torches, braziers, and arcane sconces.
Colossal natural pillars stretched from floor to ceiling,
and many of the homes carved into their bases
had channeled their chimneys directly into the stone.
High on the cavern walls,
elegant elven dwellings hung like swallows’ nests,
their delicate Art Nouveau facades
a striking counterpoint
to the angular, Art Deco severity
of the dwarven architecture that dominated the disk.
Here and there, rivulets of fresh water
wound between the structures,
feeding small but astonishing gardens
that thrived under the sustained warmth
of permanent *Daylight* enchantments.

The Auger Trail continued its descent
into the deeper levels of the city.
As they passed the junction,
a group of soot-streaked miners
trudged upward from the lower disks,
pickaxes slung over their shoulders
and lanterns swinging at their belts.

Five or six rotations into the Volition Disk,
their escorts turned off the trail
and led them into the Broken Stool.

## The Broken Stool

The tavern was a wide, comfortable establishment
carved directly into the cavern wall.
Fifteen sturdy tables filled the room,
of which only a handful were occupied—
a scattering of miners,
a pair of local merchants,
and a solitary dwarf
slumped in a corner booth, fast asleep.
The air was thick with the mingled sounds
of Common, Dwarvish, and Elvish conversation.

Behind the bar stood Luda,
the inn’s proprietress:
an older female dwarf
with grey-brown hair pulled into a severe bun,
a few defiant strands flying loose.
She wore impressive mutton-chop sideburns
and bore old, faded scars on her chin and clavicle,
visible above her folded arms.
Her expression, upon receiving the Glassblade escort’s explanation,
softened from suspicion to a gruff, wordless sympathy.

The party ordered stew and ale—
fifteen silver for the lot,
though Kragor pressed two gold pieces into Luda’s calloused palm—
and collapsed into chairs
with the particular bonelessness
of the profoundly exhausted.
As she served them,
the innkeeper’s curiosity proved sharper than her stew.
Luda let fall a series of carefully casual questions—
what manner of travelers warranted a Glassblade escort
at this hour of the night?
Had they come far?
Had anything happened on the road?
The party, mindful of Ironfell’s injunction,
offered little beyond tired smiles and grateful nods.
Luda accepted their reticence
with the practiced grace of a woman
long accustomed to tight-lipped patrons,
and shifted to gossip of her own:
tensions between the Kryn Dynasty and the Dwendalian Empire
had been escalating of late,
she confided,
with rumors of outright war circulating
through the city’s lower disks.

Several off-duty Glassblades entered shortly after,
looking nearly as haggard as the adventurers themselves.
Kragor attempted to draw Therin into conversation,
probing gently for the dragonborn’s origins.
Therin confirmed that he hailed from Draconia,
but offered little else,
maintaining the stoic reserve
the party was quickly learning to expect.

A young, red-haired adolescent dwarf
delivered their meals
and nearly dropped the tray upon catching sight of Elara.
The boy stared openly at the bard’s celestial features—
the faint luminescence, the unearthly symmetry—
with the unguarded astonishment of someone
who had clearly not encountered many aasimar.
Recovering himself with an embarrassed cough,
he offered that he knew of one other person
with a similar look about her—
a halo and everything, he said—
who worked at the smithy down on the Liber Disk.
Elara’s interest was visibly piqued.

After supper, Therin inquired about bathing facilities
and was directed to the warm baths
on the next level down, the Liber Disk,
heated by the mountain’s deep geothermal veins.

The party retired to their windowless rooms for the night.
Kragor, ever vigilant,
warded the doors of two adjoining chambers with *Alarm* rituals.
Before the candles were doused,
Elara cast *Invisibility* on Doctor Pepe,
who slipped out to conduct
what the rogue diplomatically termed “reconnaissance.”
His survey of the hallways and common room
yielded nothing of consequence,
and the invisible rogue eventually returned,
slightly deflated, to his own bed.

## The Deliberation Disk

The party woke
to the aroma of fresh bread
and the rhythmic percussion of a distant anvil—
a resonant, ceaseless hammering
that the locals fondly called
the heartbeat of Uthodurn.
Somewhere beyond the stone walls,
the lilting strains of elven music
drifted through the warm subterranean air.

Downstairs, Luda informed them
that a pair of Glassblades had stopped by at dawn.
Their presence was not required until just past midday—
the appointed hour marked, in Uthodurn’s timeless caverns,
by the shifting of arcane sun-globes
from one side of the main thoroughfare to the other.
Until then, their time was their own.

Over a breakfast of tea and coffee,
the party bombarded Luda with questions
about the city’s commercial offerings.
The innkeeper, warming perceptibly to her eager guests,
rattled off a series of recommendations:
Padillia’s emporium on the Liber Disk
for arms and provisions;
the Anvil of Smeltborne for fine metalwork;
the Plexus Post for antiques and curiosities;
Oltgar’s Chest for novelties;
and the Watersmith Baths for the weary of body and spirit.
Having reserved their rooms for another night,
the adventurers set out to explore.

The streets of the Volition Disk were now bathed
in the soft, steady glow of arcane sun-lamps
that lined the broader avenues,
lending the cavernous city an illusion
of perpetual, golden late afternoon.
The party descended the Auger Trail
to the Deliberation Disk—
oft called the “Liber Disk” for short—
where the bulk of the city’s commerce
hummed and clattered.

## The Anvil of Smeltborne

Elara and Kragor made directly
for the Anvil of Smeltborne,
drawn by the rising waves of heat
and the relentless, musical clangor of hammer on metal.
The smithy was a grand, open workshop—
kilns roaring, tables laden
with gauntlets, tools, sheets of iron, and blank metal forms—
yet what caught the eye most immediately
was the improbable profusion of fresh flowers
arranged in every available nook and crevice.

At the forge’s center labored a young dwarf
with long blonde hair tightly braided,
a magnificent mustache plaited to match,
and not a whisker upon his chin.
This was Umagorn Smeltborne,
master of the establishment
and, as Elara swiftly discovered,
entirely susceptible to aasimar charm.

Umagorn’s eyes widened
as he took in the bard’s celestial features.
He mentioned that he knew of one other aasimar—
or at least, he believed her to be one.
A woman named Reani,
who lived nearby
and was, in fact, responsible
for the smithy’s abundant floral decoration.
Umagorn spoke of her warmly:
a heart of gold, he said,
though very serious in temperament.

Turning to matters of business,
Kragor drew the magic longsword
he had recovered from the Aeorian ruins
and laid it across the counter.
Umagorn’s eyes lit up.
The smith turned the blade over in his practiced hands,
running a calloused thumb along the flat
and tilting the edge to catch the firelight.
“Interesting,” he murmured.
“This has never been struck by a hammer.”
He explained that the blade appeared to have been shaped
by some arcane or alchemical process entirely unknown to him—
no forge marks, no grind lines,
no evidence of any metalworking technique he could name.

Kragor nodded appreciatively
but confessed that, fine as the weapon was,
he preferred “a more honest weapon”—
a warhammer, enchanted to match
the longsword’s potency.
Umagorn considered the proposition,
weighing the Aeorian curiosity against the commission.
He agreed that with the longsword as trade-in,
he could forge an enchanted warhammer
at a discounted price of two hundred gold.

Kragor was not finished.
He also inquired about an elven chain shirt,
which Umagorn quoted at two hundred gold,
with the option of weaving remorhaz scales
into the mesh for an additional two hundred
to grant resistance to fire.
Kragor counted out three hundred gold
as a down payment on the lot.

Umagorn, warming to his customers,
produced several other fine weapons from his stock,
each bearing some measure of enchantment.
Kragor examined them with appreciation
and then murmured the syllables of a *Message* cantrip,
reaching out across the city
to Therin, who was at that moment
submerged to the chin in the Watersmith Baths.
The dragonborn’s reply was succinct:
he desired an enchanted mace.
Kragor relayed the commission to Umagorn
and left two hundred and fifty gold in deposit
against the mace’s full price of five hundred.
All three pieces would require a fortnight’s work.

As they prepared to leave,
Umagorn gestured toward the rear of the forge,
where a slender young elf named Deilin
worked the bellows and beat ingots
with arms that belied her willowy frame.
She was mute, Umagorn explained,
but heard and understood everything said around her.
Kragor and Elara nodded a polite greeting as they departed;
Deilin returned it with a brief, steady look
and a small inclination of her head
before turning back to the anvil.

## Padillia’s

Meanwhile, Doctor Pepe and Scarlet
arrived at the imposing double-width storefront
of Padillia’s Boutique of Ample Armaments
and Boutique of Bountiful Provisions—
a single establishment split neatly down the middle,
one half bristling with weaponry,
the other stacked with adventuring supplies.

Padillia herself presided over the chaos:
a tall, broad-shouldered attalwen
with copper-colored hair
and a faint but undeniable five o’clock shadow.
Upon hearing Doctor Pepe’s request
for new studded leather armor,
she bellowed for her assistant,
a fastidious dwarf named Borric,
who descended upon the rogue
with measuring tape, chalk, and an air of grave professionalism.
The fitting that ensued
was an exercise in meticulous indignity,
ending with an order for studded leather at forty-five gold.
Not content to stop there,
Doctor Pepe acquired a full climber’s kit—
pitons, hammer, vest, grappling hook, and silk rope—
for thirty gold,
along with chalk and a hand mirror for five more.

Scarlet, browsing with more discernment,
was measured for her own suit of studded leather
and began examining the shop’s more rarefied stock.
Borric, sensing a serious buyer,
escorted the druid to a roped-off alcove
where the establishment’s finer wares were displayed.
There, Scarlet’s practiced eye
fell upon a cloak of protection,
a pair of brass goggles enchanted to pierce the dark,
and several potions of healing.
She assembled her purchases with quiet efficiency:
studded leather armor, the cloak, the goggles,
and three healing potions,
the total of nine hundred and ninety-five gold
negotiated deftly down to nine hundred and seventy.

Doctor Pepe, drawn irresistibly to a display case,
selected a pair of finely made green boots of elvenkind
and a shortsword that hummed faintly with enchantment.
With five enchanted crossbow bolts added to the pile,
his total of eight hundred and sixty gold
was similarly haggled to eight hundred and twenty.

## The Minister

The party returned to the Broken Stool
as the arcane sun-globes completed their slow migration
across the cavern thoroughfare,
signaling just past midday.
Two Glassblades were already waiting outside.
Without ceremony, the guards escorted the party
down the Auger Trail to the Grand Disk,
Uthodurn’s deepest and most prestigious level.

Here, the cavern walls were polished to a mirror sheen.
The homes carved into the rock were grander,
more refined in their ornamentation,
and the light was softer,
emanating from elegant sconces
rather than the workmanlike sun-lamps of the upper levels.
At the far end of the disk,
the grand palace of the Diarchy loomed—
a monumental facade of carved stone and mithral filigree.

Commander Ironfell met them at the entrance
and led them not to the palace itself
but to an administrative wing,
through a succession of guarded corridors,
and finally into a long chamber of polished stone.
A detailed map of the Greying Wildlands
dominated one wall,
and a brazier in the corner
added its warmth to the scent of ink and parchment
that permeated the room.

At the head of a long, formal table—
the sort used for council meetings,
its dark surface bare save for a few sheaves of parchment—
sat an elven woman
whose silver hair was pulled back with severe precision.
The symbol of the Diarchy was embroidered
upon the breast of her formal robe,
and ink stains marked her slender fingers.
She did not stand as they entered.

“Sit,” said Minister Ërethyn Galewing.

They sat.

“I authorized that caravan,”
she continued, her voice level and precise.
“And I authorized you to protect it.
What happened?”

The party recounted the ambush in full—
the eruption of the purple worm,
the shadow-trailing ghasts,
the silent rider on the Moorbounder
who had cut down Therin and seized the artifact
with terrifying efficiency.
Therin produced the surviving lockbox,
returning the enchanted dagger
and the grapefruit-sized Aeorian orb
that had been stored in the smaller coffer.

Galewing listened without interruption.
When they had finished,
she shared that
Uthodurnian scouts had long established
that the Kryn Dynasty employed purple worms
as instruments of subterranean warfare,
and Moorbounders were common mounts
throughout Xhorhas and the Dynasty’s territories.
However, she noted a troubling inconsistency:
the Kryn did not abide undead,
yet the shadowghasts and the rider were unmistakably
creatures of necromantic origin.

“And how,” she added,
her gaze sharpening,
“did they know precisely where and when
to ambush you?”

The question hung in the warm, lamplit air.
The party had no answer.
She asked them to report anything further they might learn.

Turning to the matter of compensation,
the Minister counted out seventy-five gold to each of them—
the promised fee for the escort mission,
paid in full despite its catastrophic outcome.
She then produced a single brass disk
inlaid with silver, mithral, and copper,
bearing the Uthodurnian crest.
This token, she explained,
marked them as Friends of the Court—
a designation that would extend
certain courtesies and privileges within the city.
At Padillia’s, for instance,
the disk might secure them a discount
on clothing suitable for a court appearance.
Kragor accepted the token on behalf of the party.

At a loss for anything more to inquire
regarding the incident,
Kragor asked about the Mawcotters.
Galewing recommended the Vellum Steeple,
Uthodurn’s great archive and center of scholarship,
as the best place to research
the flesh-eating cult and their frozen patron.

Scarlet pressed for details
about the stolen artifact itself.
The Minister weighed her words carefully.
The information was closely held, she said,
but this much she could share:
the scholars of the Vellum Steeple
had not yet determined the artifact’s precise nature.
What made it exceptional
was that it was the first Aeorian relic
whose residual magic actively disrupted
the teleportation network
normally used to transport such items to Uthodurn—
hence the need for the overland caravan
that had proven so disastrously vulnerable.

Galewing acknowledged that the party’s earlier service—
the recovery and delivery of the Frigid Woe
and its antidote from Salsvault—
had been of significant value to the Diarchy,
ensuring the plague would not fall
into the hands of rival powers.

Before dismissing them,
Commander Ironfell, who had been listening in silence,
spoke up with a question of his own:
from which direction had the attackers come?
Doctor Pepe and Elara conferred briefly
and agreed that the ambush had originated from the east.

Her parting request was delivered
with the diplomatic precision of a closing door:
she asked the adventurers
not to pursue the matter of the stolen artifact further.
The Diarchy wished to handle the situation
with appropriate delicacy.

The party was escorted out
to the edge of the Liber Disk,
where one of the accompanying Glassblades
quietly encouraged them
to check in on Brennik Coalvein.
The young recruit’s quarters,
the guard mentioned,
were on the Volition Disk,
near the copper mines.
His mentor Dolgrim’s death, the guard implied,
had hit the boy hard.

## The Plexus Post

Finding themselves back on the Liber Disk
with the remainder of the day before them,
the party made their way
to the Plexus Post—
a large, gaudily decorated building
festooned with paper lanterns
crammed into every conceivable nook.
The interior presented itself
as the well-organized collection
of a deeply committed hoarder:
scented candles and dried cedar,
cured meats and old leather,
frankincense and myrrh,
all arranged with a logic
that was evident to the proprietress alone.

That proprietress was Ava Endlewood,
a woman of wide shoulders
and fine, long grey-green hair
matched by a faint grey-green mustache.
She was dressed in a patterned frock of Marquesian make,
layered beneath a loose cotton robe,
and she regarded the party
with the shrewd, knowing gaze
of someone who had heard every negotiating tactic
at least twice.

Doctor Pepe expressed interest
in stocking a poisoner’s kit.
Ava raised an unsurprised eyebrow
and produced wolfsbane, dragonsbane,
and several other noxious botanicals
from beneath her counter.
But she admitted that the shop could not provide
the equipment required to transform these
into poison.

Scarlet asked about magical items.
Ava gestured airily toward the higher shelves,
where an Amulet of Protection from Scrying
sat tagged at three thousand five hundred gold,
and a Bag of Holding at an eye-watering four thousand.

Kragor purchased a pearl
worth one hundred gold,
a necessary component for his *Identify* ritual.
Emboldened by the transaction,
and perhaps by some deeper impulse,
the warlock attempted a more creative exchange—
offering a vial of Frigid Woe
for the Bag of Holding,
sweetening the pitch with an undetectable invocation of *Suggestion*.
Ava’s eyes glazed for a moment,
then cleared with a counteroffer:
bring her a Yeti pelt,
and she would consider a trade.
She then leaned in conspiratorially
and mentioned that she might,
from time to time,
have need of individuals willing to acquire items
through channels less conventional than purchase.
Doctor Pepe, who had been browsing a nearby shelf
with studied indifference,
caught the implication immediately
and offered a subtle, knowing nod
that conveyed his fluency in the language being spoken.

Therin, who had largely observed the proceedings
with the quiet patience of the devout,
asked whether the shop stocked
any symbols of Bahamut.
Ava produced a small, folded ladder from within her robes
and set it against the nearest shelf.
As she climbed, the rungs extended beneath her feet,
the contraption stretching impossibly upward
until she reached the high shelf she required.
She descended with a platinum-on-bronze holy symbol,
priced at fifty gold.
The dragonborn bought it without hesitation.

Stepping back into the warm, lamp-lit streets of the Liber Disk,
the party fell into an easy discussion of what remained to be done.
Doctor Pepe and Scarlet's enthusiastic accounts
of Padillia's stock had piqued the interest
of those who had not yet visited the emporium.
Elara, who alone among the party
had not yet spent a single coin,
declared her intention to rectify this oversight,
and Kragor expressed a desire to browse as well.
After that, they agreed,
they would make their way up to the Volition Disk
to check in on Brennik.
The young recruit had lost his mentor
and very nearly his life,
and the least they could do
was see how the boy was holding up.
